What I Wish I Knew When I Started in Cybersecurity

When I first stepped into the world of cybersecurity, I was filled with excitement and curiosity. The idea of protecting systems, uncovering vulnerabilities, and outsmarting attackers was thrilling. But like most journeys, it wasn’t without its surprises. If I could go back in time and share some advice with my younger self, these are the lessons I’d pass along.

1. Learn to Manage Expectations

Cybersecurity isn’t as black and white as it seems from the outside. When I started, I thought every vulnerability could be patched, every attack could be stopped, and every workflow could be streamlined. The reality? There’s always a trade-off. Whether it’s between speed and thoroughness or between usability and security, you’ll constantly be navigating gray areas.

2. It’s Not Just About Tech

I thought cybersecurity would be all about tools, scripts, and systems. While those are essential, the real challenges often lie in communication and collaboration. You’ll spend just as much time explaining security concepts to non-technical stakeholders as you will deploying technical solutions. Learning to speak the language of business and leadership is just as important as mastering the latest tools.

3. Automation is Your Friend

Early on, I wasted hours on repetitive tasks—manually combing through logs, running scans, and compiling reports. I wish I had known sooner just how powerful automation can be. Tools like Python scripts, Cron jobs, and AI-assisted analysis aren’t just nice-to-haves; they’re essential for staying ahead in this field.

4. Politics Matter

Cybersecurity exists within the context of an organization, and organizations are made up of people. It’s not enough to have the right answer or the best solution—you have to navigate the politics of resource allocation, priorities, and team dynamics. Building relationships and understanding the bigger picture can make or break your success.

5. Continuous Learning is Non-Negotiable

This field moves fast. What’s cutting-edge today might be obsolete tomorrow. I’ve learned to stay curious and embrace a mindset of constant growth. Whether it’s learning about AI, diving into new compliance standards, or experimenting with the latest tools, the key is staying adaptable.

6. Mistakes Are Part of the Process

When I started, I was terrified of making mistakes. But in cybersecurity, errors are inevitable—whether it’s overlooking a vulnerability or deploying the wrong fix. The important thing is how you respond. Every mistake is an opportunity to learn, improve, and become better at your craft.

7. You’re Not Alone

Cybersecurity can feel isolating, especially when you’re handling sensitive issues or trying to convince leadership to take action. I wish I had known how important it is to build a support network. Whether it’s mentors, colleagues, or online communities, having people to share insights, challenges, and successes with makes all the difference.

Final Thoughts

If I could sum up all these lessons, it would be this: cybersecurity is about more than just stopping threats. It’s about solving problems, collaborating with others, and constantly adapting to a changing landscape. The path isn’t always easy, but it’s one of the most rewarding fields out there.

To anyone just starting their journey in cybersecurity: stay curious, stay persistent, and don’t be afraid to fail. You’ll learn more than you ever imagined—and you might just find yourself exactly where you need to be.
